Chapter 01 · The Seed

It started with a feeling of irrelevance.

The previous project — codenamed Birds View — was a generic visual second-brain app: a blank canvas, draggable cards, mood boards, notes. It worked. But the market was already full of Milanote, Notion, Mural, Pinterest. The product was fine; the wedge into the market was not. We shelved it.

The new idea arrived from a very specific place: my cousin's architecture firm in Pune. Watching how he ran his practice — fifteen projects across four cities, ten architects under him, a dozen WhatsApp groups, a Google Drive nobody could navigate — it was clear there was a real workflow nobody had built software for.

But before drawing a single screen, one question needed an honest answer: does this product already exist?

02
Chapter 02 · The Market

Most of what I had in mind already exists.

Three weeks of teardown later, the answer was: yes, painfully. The visual-canvas-for-architects concept is not new. The moodboard part, the timeline part, the document shelf — all of these exist, and several already combined. Senior UX work begins with the willingness to say that out loud.

Not one of these tools is built for how an Indian solo-principal architect actually runs a practice. None model RERA compliance milestones, municipal corporation approval stages, or the structural-consultant handoff workflow. The localization gap was real — and large.

The competitor isn't another app. It's WhatsApp, Google Drive, Excel, and the principal's memory.

— Insight from market analysis, week 3

03
Chapter 03 · The Pivot

From visual canvas to painkiller.

The original idea pitched a "second brain on a canvas" — beautiful, but a vitamin. Nice to have, easy to live without. Vitamins don't sell to Indian small businesses, who are notoriously rational about software spend. The product needed to be a painkiller: something a principal architect cannot live without by week three.

What architects say they need
  • Beautiful mood boards
  • Project visualization
  • Client presentations
  • Portfolio organization
What actually loses them money
  • Material decisions lost in WhatsApp scroll
  • Carpenters building from outdated drawing versions
  • Client meetings where the principal is unprepared
  • Structural consultant delays nobody tracks

This was the wedge. The mood boards stay — they are the seductive surface, the reason a creative person opens the app at all. But the engine room sits underneath: material decisions logged with photos and approvals, drawing versions controlled with one always-pinned "latest approved," and a morning brief that summarizes what to ask, what to show, what to decide before every client meeting.

Chapter 05 · The Business Shape

Priced for the way Indian SMBs actually buy.

Western architecture-firm SaaS averages $40–60 per seat per month. Translating that directly to India is a death sentence. Indian small-firm software pricing has different physics: flat per-firm, not per-seat. Affordable enough to never require a procurement conversation. The principal pays from her personal card and forwards the invoice to her accountant on Diwali eve.

Studio · Tier 1
₹1,500 /month

For practices up to 10 architects. The principal buys, adds her team by email.

  • Unlimited projects & mood boards
  • Drawing version control
  • Material decisions log
  • Real-time team activity
  • Mobile + desktop access
Studio · Tier 2
₹2,000 /month

For practices of 10 to 50 architects. Same product, more seats, finer permissions.

  • Everything in Tier 1
  • Role-based permissions (PM / Lead / Intern)
  • RERA compliance module (add-on)
  • Custom firm branding on client exports
  • Priority support

At a blended ARPU around ₹20,000/year, the math to a meaningful ₹2–3 Cr ARR business needs ~1,000–1,500 paying firms. The realistic TAM of Indian solo & small architecture practices in tier-1 cities is roughly 30–50,000 firms. A healthy bootstrappable SMB SaaS company — not a venture rocket, not a side project.

Chapter 06 · The Honest Decision

The hardest part: deciding not to build yet.

The mockups were ready. Architect friends were waiting. The temptation to start coding was real. But the most senior UX move at this point is also the least Instagrammable: stop, and validate.

The Birds View project's failure wasn't a design failure. It was a sequencing failure — built before validated. To not repeat that, a four-week research plan was designed before committing to a single line of code.

01
Recruit 20 architects
Mix of cousin's network, LinkedIn outreach to strangers in Pune, Mumbai, Bangalore — and alumni from architecture colleges.
02
Run video interviews
25 minutes each. Eight questions. Show mockups only at the end. Listen 80%, talk 20%. Record with permission.
03
Find the patterns
Which 3 pains show up in 80% of conversations? Which features made eyes light up? Which made them glaze over?
04
Make the call
If 8+ architects say "when can I have this" unprompted — build. If they nod politely — kill it. Either is a win.

Killing an idea in week 4 is better than killing it in week 40. The graveyard is full of beautifully-designed Indian SaaS products built without this conversation. Choosing — for now — to be in the graveyard's negative, not its positive.

07
Closing · Reflections

What this project taught me
about being a designer.

The interface work is the visible 10%. The other 90% is being honest about whether the problem is even worth solving — and being willing to say not yet.

— Personal reflection, Atelier 2026

01
Beautiful mockups are not validation.
A polished design can convince anyone — including its designer — that the product is real. Validation only comes from strangers saying yes with their wallets.
02
Friends are not your customers.
Your friends will be polite. They'll say "great, send me the link" and never use it. Strangers paid ₹500 in an Amazon voucher will be ten times more honest.
03
Localization is a moat.
Global tools rarely build for non-Western workflows. RERA, sanctions, structural handoffs — these are real product surfaces nobody has touched.
04
Painkiller, not vitamin.
People pay for things that prevent loss far more than for things that add delight. Recast every feature: is this a vitamin or a painkiller?
05
Distribution > product.
The hardest part of Indian SaaS isn't building the software. It's finding the first 50 customers. Designers ignore this layer at their peril.
06
The brave thing is to stop.
Knowing when to pause is a senior skill. Most failed startups didn't fail because of bad design. They failed because nobody stopped to ask the harder question.
