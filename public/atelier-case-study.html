The Context

One principal.
Eight architects.
Fifteen WhatsApp groups.

This is the Indian solo-principal architecture firm. One senior architect — the principal — runs the practice, owns the client relationships, makes every creative call, and is simultaneously a project manager, salesperson, and design director.

Their team of 6–10 architects work across multiple live projects. The drawing is on the wall. The client meeting is in an hour. The structural consultant hasn't replied in 9 days. And somewhere in a WhatsApp scroll from three weeks ago is the tile choice the client approved.

A typical morning for AR. R. SHARMA
08:45 Opens 3 WhatsApp groups to find yesterday's tile approval from Mrs. Mehta. Scrolls 40 messages back.
09:15 Checks Google Drive for the latest Sethi drawing. Finds three files named "GFC_Final_v3_FINAL_USE_THIS.dwg"
10:00 Client meeting in 6 hours. Still unsure which tile sample to bring. Hasn't confirmed with carpenter about start date.
10:30 Opens Atelier. One sentence tells him everything. Three cards tell him exactly what needs attention today.

Chapter 01 · The Seed

It started with a feeling of irrelevance.

The previous project — codenamed Birds View — was a generic visual second-brain app: a blank canvas, draggable cards, mood boards, notes. It worked. But the market was already full of Milanote, Notion, Mural, Pinterest. The product was fine; the wedge into the market was not. We shelved it.

The new idea arrived from a very specific place: my cousin's architecture firm in Pune. Watching how he ran his practice — fifteen projects across four cities, ten architects under him, a dozen WhatsApp groups, a Google Drive nobody could navigate — it was clear there was a real workflow nobody had built software for.

But before drawing a single screen, one question needed an honest answer: does this product already exist?

Chapter 02 · The Market

Most of what I had in mind already exists.

Three weeks of teardown later, the answer was: yes, painfully. The visual-canvas-for-architects concept is not new. All of it exists. Senior UX work begins with the willingness to say that out loud.

Chapter 03 · The Pivot

From visual canvas to painkiller.

The original idea pitched a "second brain on a canvas" — beautiful, but a vitamin. Nice to have, easy to live without. The product needed to be a painkiller: something a principal architect cannot live without by week three.

What architects say they need
  • Beautiful mood boards
  • Project visualization
  • Client presentations
  • Portfolio organization
What actually loses them money
  • Material decisions lost in WhatsApp scroll
  • Carpenters building from outdated drawing versions
  • Client meetings where the principal is unprepared
  • Structural consultant delays nobody tracks

The mood boards stay — they are the seductive surface. But the engine room sits underneath: material decisions logged with photos, drawing versions controlled, and a morning brief that tells the principal exactly what to ask and decide before every client meeting.

Chapter 06 · The Honest Decision

The hardest part: deciding not to build yet.

The mockups were ready. Architect friends were waiting. The temptation to start coding was real. But the most senior UX move at this point is also the least Instagrammable: stop, and validate.

The Birds View failure wasn't a design failure. It was a sequencing failure — built before validated. A four-week research plan was designed before committing to a single line of code.

01
Recruit 20 architects
Mix of network, LinkedIn outreach to strangers in Pune, Mumbai, Bangalore — and architecture college alumni.
02
Run video interviews
25 minutes each. Eight questions. Show mockups only at the end. Listen 80%, talk 20%. Record with permission.
03
Find the patterns
Which 3 pains show up in 80% of conversations? Which features made eyes light up? Which made them glaze over?
04
Make the call
If 8+ architects say "when can I have this" unprompted — build. If they nod politely — kill it. Either is a win.

Killing an idea in week 4 is better than killing it in week 40. The graveyard is full of beautifully-designed Indian SaaS products built without this conversation.

Chapter 04 · The Screens

The live prototypes.
Every screen, actual scale.

Three fully interactive screens — built and live. Not static mockups. The actual working UI.

Screen 01 / The Canvas

The morning brief.
The whole firm. Five seconds.

What the principal sees the moment they open Atelier with their first coffee. One sentence summarises the day. Active projects float as slightly-tilted cards on a grid that feels like a studio wall — not a SaaS dashboard. The right panel shows exactly what decision is blocking which project right now.

  • Hero brief — the single most important sentence of the day, auto-generated
  • Floating project cards with phase-strips: done/now/pending at a glance
  • Right panel: decisions needed + upcoming client meetings with prep notes
  • Revenue this quarter without opening a single spreadsheet
  • Badges surface blockers, delays, and deadlines before the principal even asks
Screen 02 / Inside a Project

A moodboard wall,
with the whole team baked in.

Click a project card and you enter its own canvas. Four tabs — Vision (the moodboard), Drawings (version-controlled), Decisions (every material logged with photo and approval), Timeline (phases, vendors, dependencies). Every pinned item carries the avatar of who added it. The right panel shows who's online and what they just changed.

  • Eight pin types: images, references, color palettes, materials, sticky notes, drawings
  • Live activity feed — see team movement in real time without asking
  • Client brief lives on the canvas as a pin, not buried in settings
  • Slight-tilt cards mirror a physical moodboard — intentional, not accidental
  • Principal vs team-member view: same canvas, different level of detail
Screen 03 / The Library

Two hundred projects.
Still scannable.

When the firm has 200 projects across 8 years, the canvas alone doesn't hold up. The Library surfaces only what needs the principal today — a smart attention strip at the top — then shows every project ever, filterable by status, type, city, project manager, fee bracket, or payment state.

  • "Needs attention today" strip — the system does the triage for you
  • Distinct Indian status types: "Reopened — modification," "Sanction only"
  • Grid, Canvas, List, and Map views — switch without losing context
  • Map view: all projects across Pune, Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore on one screen
  • Compact cards maintain the visual rhythm of the original canvas

Closing · Reflections

What this taught me
about being a designer.

The interface work is the visible 10%. The other 90% is being honest about whether the problem is even worth solving — and being willing to say not yet.

— Personal reflection, Atelier 2026

01
Beautiful mockups are not validation.
A polished design can convince anyone — including its designer — that the product is real. Validation only comes from strangers saying yes with their wallets.
02
Friends are not your customers.
Your friends will be polite. They'll say "great, send me the link" and never use it. Strangers paid ₹500 in an Amazon voucher will be ten times more honest.
03
Localization is a moat.
Global tools rarely build for non-Western workflows. RERA, sanctions, structural handoffs — real product surfaces nobody has touched.
04
Painkiller, not vitamin.
People pay for things that prevent loss far more than for things that add delight. Recast every feature: is this a vitamin or a painkiller?
05
Distribution > product.
The hardest part of Indian SaaS isn't building the software. It's finding the first 50 customers. Designers ignore this layer at their peril.
06
The brave thing is to stop.
Knowing when to pause is a senior skill. Most failed startups didn't fail because of bad design. They failed because nobody stopped to ask the harder question.
