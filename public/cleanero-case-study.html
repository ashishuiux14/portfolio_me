01
The Problem

You can't trust
what you can't see.

Real user frustration

"Is this water actually safe to drink?"

Traditional filter pitchers — Brita, Pur, ZeroWater — all solve the filtration problem. But none of them solve the trust problem.

Users have zero visibility into TDS levels, actual filter health, or whether their water is clean right now. They get a generic "replace every 2 months" sticker — regardless of how much water they've filtered or how hard their tap water actually is.

Students in shared housing were skipping their own pitchers and buying bottled water instead — not because the water was bad, but because they had no way to know.

03
Research Process

Understanding the gap
between trust and reality.

Finding 01
Trust was already broken
Multiple users had stopped using their pitchers entirely, defaulting to bottled water. Not because the filter failed — but because they assumed it might have.
67% uncertain about filter status
Finding 02
Generic timelines don't match real life
"Replace every 2 months" ignores usage volume, water hardness, and local TDS levels. High-use households needed replacement in weeks, not months.
3× variance in actual filter lifespan
Finding 03
A number changes everything
When shown a real TDS reading during testing, users immediately engaged and formed habits around checking it. Visibility didn't just inform — it changed behavior.
89% changed habits after seeing live TDS
Finding 04
Language was the hidden barrier
Mid-fi testing revealed "Initiate TDS Test" caused hesitation and confusion. Renaming it to simply "Measure" increased task completion by 34%. UX copy is UX design.
+34% task completion after rename
Research synthesis

From frustration to design direction

Our research started from a personal place — shared frustration with filtered pitchers that looked clean but offered no proof of it. Desk research, legal reports (including a Brita lawsuit), and customer reviews confirmed this wasn't unique to us.

The pattern was universal: lack of transparency in filter performance leads directly to low trust, inconsistent maintenance, and — ultimately — people choosing bottled water over a perfectly functional pitcher sitting right in front of them.

04
Personas & Insights

Real people,
real disconnects.

Key persona insight

The product and user had drifted apart

We observed users in shared kitchens interacting with their pitchers. Many admitted they hadn't replaced filters in months — citing forgetfulness, or simply not knowing when replacement was actually needed.

Some avoided the pitcher entirely, opting for bottled water out of mistrust. This wasn't a product failure — it was a trust failure. The product worked. The user just had no way to know it.

The opportunity: frame Cleaneró as a personal water health assistant — not just a filter pitcher. That's a positioning no competitor owned.
05
Design Process

Sketch. Test.
Simplify. Repeat.

Step 01
Concept Sketching
Explored how TDS data could be displayed on both the physical pitcher and the app. Focused on screen placement, touch interactions, and visual hierarchy.
Step 02
Lo-fi Wireframes
Mapped onboarding, TDS measurement, and filter replacement flows. Established key metrics and CTAs at the top of the visual hierarchy.
Step 03
User Testing
Mid-fi prototype testing revealed language confusion and navigation issues. Simplified terminology, added filter health icons, made CTAs context-aware.
Step 04
Hi-Fi & Handoff
Clean, calm, confident visual language. Blue data, white surfaces. Physical-digital consistency across pitcher display and mobile app.
