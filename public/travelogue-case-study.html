01
The Problem

Travel planning is
scattered everywhere.

Core frustration

Google Docs. WhatsApp. Spreadsheets. None of it talks to each other.

Group travel planning is a mess of copy-pasted links, endless threads, and someone always missing the update. There's no single place where the plan, the people, and the memories live together.

And once the trip ends — all of it disappears. No dedicated space to look back, reflect, or share the story of where you went.

Current apps offer itinerary planning or social storytelling. Travelogue's entire purpose is the space between those two things.

02
Research Process

Three methods.
One clear direction.

A multi-method approach — interviews, card sorting, and usability testing — that didn't just validate assumptions. It actively reshaped the product.

11
User Interviews
Ages 18–35. Solo and group travelers. Key finding: collaboration mattered more than gamification — which completely reprioritised the feature set.
6
Card Sorting Sessions
Open card sorting via Optimal Workshop revealed how users mentally group features — directly shaping the bottom nav and three primary sections.
6
Usability Tests
Mid-fi and hi-fi testing uncovered confusing CTAs, invite discoverability issues, and offline gaps — all fixed in iteration.
🤝
Collaboration over gamification
Users consistently prioritised co-editing plans with friends over earning badges. Gamification dropped from core feature to light secondary layer.
Research pivot
📶
Offline access is non-negotiable
Travelers going abroad or into remote areas cited offline access as critical. This became a dedicated "Make Available Offline" toggle per trip — not a nice-to-have.
Core feature driven by research
🗂️
Card sorting rewrote navigation
Users drew clear mental distinctions between "planning before the trip" and "experiences during." This restructured the entire bottom nav into Trip Planning, On-Trip, and Explore.
Navigation redesigned
✏️
Onboarding needed honesty
Users didn't understand how interest selections personalised their experience. Adding live example tooltips mid-onboarding solved the confusion immediately in testing.
Usability fix

People valued collaboration more than gamification. That one finding changed the entire product priority.

— Key insight, user interviews · Travelogue 2025

03
Personas

Who we were
designing for.

Persona insight

Three types of traveler. One consistent frustration.

Whether solo adventurer, group organiser, or casual weekend tripper — every persona hit the same wall. Too many tools, too much friction, and no way to capture what the trip actually felt like once it was over.

The group organiser was doing the work of a project manager — inside WhatsApp. That's not a user problem. That's a product gap.

The "group organiser" persona was spending more time coordinating than actually planning. Travelogue needed to make them feel like a host, not an admin.
Research → Design
Navigation Structure
Card sorting revealed mental groupings → restructured bottom nav into 3 clear sections
Offline-First Mode
Cited as crucial across all interviews → dedicated toggle per trip
Visual Hierarchy
Users prioritised checklists + docs over social → elevated in dashboard
Onboarding Tooltips
Confusion about interest selections → live examples added mid-flow
