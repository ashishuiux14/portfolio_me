01
The Problem

Range anxiety is
psychological, not just technical

"Will I make it? Is that charger even working? What if I get stuck?"

— Arjun, 32. Product Manager. EV Owner. Every long drive, every time.

EV adoption is growing fast — but the mental model of long-distance driving hasn't caught up. Users have enough battery range. What they lack is trust in the system around them. That's the real design problem.

⚡
Battery % ≠ Actual Range

Weather, terrain, and driving style all erode the displayed percentage in ways users can't predict. The number lies — or at least, it feels like it does.

🔌
Charger Uncertainty

Stations may be occupied, out of service, painfully slow, or simply broken. Drivers arrive to find dead chargers with no recourse.

🧠
Cognitive Overload

Drivers juggle Maps, PlugShare, OEM apps — context-switching mid-journey. Three apps to answer one question: "Am I going to make it?"

📉
Static Information

Existing tools show you where chargers are. None predict whether they'll be available, reliable, and fast enough when you actually arrive.

02
Research Insights

Five truths users
revealed

01
Reliability beats proximity

Users consistently choose a farther charger with high reliability over a closer one with uncertain uptime. Distance is a known cost; failure is not.

78% prefer reliable over closest
02
Self-imposed buffer zones

Most users never charge below 20-25%. This isn't product design — it's coping behavior. They've given up trusting the system's stated range.

+25% the invisible minimum drivers set
03
Range is deeply personal

The same car, the same route, in the hands of two drivers yields dramatically different outcomes. Driving style, climate usage, terrain — all compound.

3× variance between aggressive and smooth drivers
04
App-switching causes danger

Drivers actively use multiple apps while in motion. The multi-app workflow isn't a feature request — it's a safety problem in disguise.

2.4× more likely to check phone mid-drive
05
Confidence, not just data

More data doesn't reduce anxiety. What users want is a system that says "you'll be fine — here's why" and means it. Certainty, not information overload.

91% want a confidence indicator, not just miles
Insight
The real gap in the market

Tesla, PlugShare, Google Maps — all focus on navigation OR charging. None connect both with real-time predictive intelligence, behavioral personalization, and risk communication.

0 tools address all five pain points together
03
Design Principles

Three pillars that
guided every decision

Pillar 01
Confidence over raw data

Don't show "You have 40 miles." Show "You can safely travel 30 miles with high confidence." The goal is never information — it's certainty.

Pillar 02
Predictive over reactive

Don't wait for something to go wrong. Think ahead. Suggest re-routes before traffic becomes a problem. Flag charger risks before the driver arrives.

Pillar 03
Glanceable, never demanding

In-car UI must be absorbed in under 2 seconds. No dashboards. No graphs mid-drive. Hierarchy so clear it can be read at 70mph.

Shift from reactive navigation → predictive mobility intelligence. Instead of "Here's your route" — answer "You will reach safely, and here's why."

— Core design philosophy, EV Trip Intelligence

04
Design Process

How the system
came to life

Step 01
Journey Mapping

End-to-end flow from trip planning through charge and continuation. Mapped anxiety peaks and decision moments at each stage.

Step 02
Information Architecture

Grouped into Navigation, Energy, Charging, and Risk — then applied progressive disclosure to eliminate clutter without hiding depth.

Step 03
Interaction Design

Mobile: one-hand interactions. In-car: glanceability-first. Voice as a universal fallback for zero-touch scenarios.

Step 04
Visual Language

Automotive-minimal. Green for safe, amber for caution, red for risk. Range visualised as bands — not raw numbers.

Calm when
things change

Traffic, charger failures, weather — the system adapts continuously and communicates changes without creating panic. Not "Alert: problem detected" — instead, "Here's what's better."

07
System Architecture

This isn't an app —
it's an ecosystem

The intelligence comes from three layers working in parallel: the mobile app for planning and remote monitoring, the in-car interface for real-time guidance, and a cloud data layer that connects battery state, charger networks, and behavioral models.

📱
Mobile App

Trip planning, remote monitoring, post-trip insights, and charger discovery. The command center before and after every journey.

🚘
In-Car Interface

Glanceable, voice-first guidance during active drives. Real-time confidence indicators, range bands, and adaptive re-routing.

☁️
Intelligence Layer

Battery telemetry, charger network feeds, behavioral models, and community data — fused into a continuous real-time prediction engine.
