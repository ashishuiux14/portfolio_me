01
The Problem

One nightstand.
Three chargers. Zero elegance.

The everyday frustration

iPhone. Apple Watch. AirPods. Each on its own cable.

Most people who own Apple devices end up with a nightstand or desk that looks like a cable graveyard. Separate docks, separate cables, separate chargers — all for devices made by the same company, designed to work together.

Integrated solutions exist — but Apple's own MagSafe stand costs a premium, and third-party alternatives sacrifice aesthetics for function. The gap: a beautiful, affordable, all-in-one dock that actually looks like it belongs on your desk.

The existing Scott Yu-Jan iPhone dock was already a great starting point. The challenge was to extend it — integrating the Apple Watch puck without compromising the original minimalist aesthetic or footprint.
Cable clutter Expensive solutions Poor aesthetics No integrated design

What if you could have Apple-level elegance without the Apple price tag? That question became a 3D printer and three weeks of iteration.

— Design premise, iPhone Charging Dock

02
Inspiration & Challenge

Max Braun's principles.
Applied to a drawer mechanism.

Design philosophy

Less, but better.

Max Braun's design principles — simplicity, functionality, and honesty of materials — guided every decision. If it didn't need to be there, it wasn't. If it existed, it did something.

The hardest constraint: integrate the Apple Watch puck without increasing the dock's footprint. This meant building a push-pull drawer mechanism — hidden when not in use, deployed in one clean motion when you need it.

The watch charger had to disappear when not in use. A push-pull mechanism from Amazon drawer fittings — after multiple spring tension iterations — became the elegant solution hiding inside a 3D printed shell.

Research & Feedback

Hypothetical + Real feedback

Shown to friends and roommates. The reaction was immediate.

Before building, the concept was validated with friends and roommates who owned iPhones and Apple Watches. The response was unanimous: everyone wanted this. The specific pain point of desk clutter resonated across the board.

The key insight from early conversations: it wasn't just about function. People wanted something they actually wanted to look at on their desk — not just a cable organiser with a premium name badge.

Everyone said yes before the first prototype was even printed. That told me the problem was real — and the market gap was wide open.

04
Design Process

Sketch. Model.
Print. Iterate.

Step 01
Concept & Sketching
Explored configurations for integrating the Watch charger. Focused on ergonomics, cable management, and how to hide the puck without adding bulk.
Step 02
3D Modelling
CAD modelling — maintaining original footprint while carving internal cavity for push-pull mechanism and cable routing.
Step 03
Mechanism Iteration
Multiple spring tensions tested. Settled on off-the-shelf Amazon drawer push-pulls — perfectly sized, cost-effective.
Step 04
Final Print & Finish
Refined tolerances. Matte filament finish to honor the Braun-influenced minimal aesthetic. Ready for the community.
🔧
Push-pull drawer mechanism
The Watch charger arm deploys and retracts via a spring-loaded push-pull mechanism — same hardware used in kitchen cabinets. No loose parts, no wobble.
📐
Original footprint preserved
The redesign adds full Apple Watch integration without increasing the dock's desk footprint. Everything hides inside the original form factor.
🪄
Cable management built-in
Internal channels route both Lightning/USB-C and Watch puck cables cleanly. No exposed wires. No velcro. No excuses.
💰
Fraction of the cost
Achieves results comparable to $200+ commercial solutions at the cost of filament and a $3 mechanism. Democratised industrial design.
